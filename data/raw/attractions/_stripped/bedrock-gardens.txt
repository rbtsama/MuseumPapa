This 30-acre site has recently transitioned from a historic farm and private garden to a public oasis of horticulture, art, and inspiration.

Giant Solomon Seal

​ Giant Solomon’s Seal; Smooth Solomon’s Seal (Polygonatum biflorum) A native woodland perennial found throughout eastern North America and Canada, giant Solomon’s seal’s common name is supposedly based on the resemblance of its root scars to the seal of the ancient King Solomon. It is usually about 3’ tall but can grow larger. Dangling clusters of edible bell-shaped white and green flowers hang from the stalk, blooming March-June. Its potato-like rhizomes were used as a food source by Native Americans and young shoots are also eaten, similar to asparagus spears. Giant Solomon’s Seal has also been extensively used as an herbal medicine. Caution should always be observed when eating wild plants; giant Solomon’s seal has a similar-looking cousin, false Solomon’s seal or Solomon’s plume (Maianthemem racemosum) that is highly bitter and largely unpalatable. The two species are most easily distinguished by their flowers. False Solomon’s seal in turn closely resembles a different highly toxic plant. At Bedrock Gardens, stands of giant Solomon’s seal can be found in the Spiral (#12), Swaleway (#14) and Funnel (#13) gardens.

Japanese Primrose

Japanese Primrose ​ ​( Primula japonica ) Sometimes called the candelabra primrose, this beautiful perennial produces blooms in whorls along its flower stalk as the stalk grows from a basal cluster of crinkled leaves . Each stalk can carry as many as 5 or 6 rings of flowers and reach 18” high. It is happiest in moist shady areas and blooms in shades of white, purple, pink, and magenta in May-June. It attracts a variety of pollinators such as butterflies, bees, and hummingbirds and will self-sow if happy. A mass of Japanese primroses in bloom is truly a memorable sight and brings bright color to shady areas. At Bedrock Gardens, Japanese primroses can be found in the Tea House and Petit Pond (#29) garden as well as in the Swaleway (#14) garden.

Mayapple

Mayapple; Podophyllum peltatum The mayapple is a woodland wildflower that makes its biggest impact with its striking leaves, which resemble umbrellas arising from a single stalk. Mayapples are native across most of the eastern US and southeastern Canada in upland forests, well-drained floodplain forests, and in many moist disturbed shaded habitats. They are hardy in many climate zones, throughout USDA zones 3-8. The stalks grow 1-1 ½’ tall and come from thick fibrous roots that arise from a creeping rhizome; this commonly results in a large spreading colony. Each year the rhizome extends 2-8” and terminates in a bud for the next year’s stalk as well as several buds for the future growth and branching of the rhizome. Leaves are large (roughly 8-16” in diameter) and palmately lobed with 5-9 deeply cut lobes. Stems with single leaves, usually less mature, do not flower while stems bearing 2 leaves produce a single flower in the axil between the leaves. As leaves first emerge in April, they initially droop downward, expanding to a horizontal position as they grow. The white waxy flowers are nodding, about 1-2” across, and have 6-9 petals. Although the flowers are quite showy, they can be short-lived and are partially hidden by the leaves. The flowers are fragrant but their scent is variously described as a pleasant mix of fruits and honey or as musky and skunky. Although the plant’s common name is “mayapple”, it is the flower that appears in May, not the fruit or “apple”. The flowers can self-pollinate but are usually pollinated by insects such as bumblebees and other long-tongued bees, butterflies, and moths which are attracted by the nutritious pollen rather than nectar, which the flowers lack. After pollination the fruit ripens through the early summer into a greenish yellow egg or lemon-shaped “apple” 1-2” across and about 2” long. After ripening, the above-ground parts of the plant die back. Timing of this dieback is affected by both the health of the rhizome system and environmental conditions, with plants in sunnier areas disappearing earlier. Aside from the ripe fruit, which can be made into jellies and jams, all parts of the mayapple are poisonous and contain a highly toxic compound called podophyllotoxin or podophyllin. Because of this, the plants are not consumed by wildlife; some small mammals and box turtles do consume the fruit. Plants however, were used by native Americans due to their biological activity in a number of ways – as an emetic, cathartic, and deworming agent. Today, podophyllotoxin and its derivatives are used in the treatment of certain cancers. Our native mayapples are close relatives to several Asian mayapple species; a linkage thought to be due to a shared ancient ancestry. Because mayapples spread rapidly and die back in summer, they can be difficult to use in many garden settings. They are however, wonderful in naturalized woodland gardens where their bold foliage adds a spring flush and they can be used to carpet the ground below deciduous trees. They are also a beautiful addition to a native wildflower garden. At Bedrock Gardens, mayapples.

Yellowroot

Yellowroot ; Xanthorhiza simpli c i ssima Yellowroot is a tough and adaptable shrub native to the US with a long history of human use s . It was o riginally found in the eastern US from Maine to Florida , and west to Ohio and Texas , in moist sandy soils along stream edges or in damp woods under partial shade. The plant derives both its common and scientific names from the distinctive coloration of its roots , caused by the presence of high concentrations of an alkaloid, berberine. Its genus “ X anthorhiza ” means yellow foot or root . Its species name “ simplicissima ” means refers to its simple (no t branched) stems . Yellowroot grows roughly 3 ’ tall and its thin stems (~ ¼ “ diameter ) giv e the plant an overall leggy appearance. The stems hold compound leaves 4-7” long arranged in a spiral , each composed of 5 serrated leaflets. Leaves resemble those of parsley or celery . The n ew spring growth has a maroon shading that changes to a bright green during the summer. The small airy f lowers appear in spring (April-May) in drooping clusters 2-8” long from the bases of the leaves at the upper part of the stems. They are interesting when viewed closely but not showy – small (up to ¼“ across ) , star-shaped with 5 petals, and reddish to purplish brown. The seeds are eaten by birds and small mammals, and the plants can provide cover for them. In addition to its seeds, y ellowroot also spreads vigorously by rhizome growth if plants are happy. As indicated by its large native range , it is hardy across a wide temperature span – throughout USDA zones 3 – 9. The plant was first described by botanists in 1773 when American William Bartram encountered it along a stream in GA, calling it “ a very curious Little Shrub ”. However, native Americans in the Appalachian region have used it for thousands of years and r esearchers have documented at least 34 different uses of the plant by them . Its utility was passed on to early colonists. High concentrations of bright yellow berberine m eant that it ha d both biological activity as a medicine well as use as a dye. It was a common dye used for baskets and feathers as well as face paint and textiles . T he dye fades to an unappealing olive green in sunlight , so that in later years it had little use in the textile industry , and so its use on fabric has declined . Berberine acts in a variety of ways in the human body and is described as being an anti-inflammatory, astringent, hemostatic, antimicrobial, anticonvulsant and immuno-stimulant agent . As such it was used in traditional medicine for a wide range of digestive and circulatory illnesses . It was used to treat heart problems, diarrhea, bloody urine, cramps , and for inducing labor. Topical preparations were used for a number of infections such as skin sores and eye infections. Teas from root extracts were used for stomach ulcers, colds, jaundice , sore throats and mouths , and thrush . And finally, t he thin stems were also used in basket making. Aside from its interesting cultural history, y ellowroot can be used in a number of ways. If plant ed in sunn ier areas, i ts attractive foliage adds interest since it can turn bright yellows and reds in the fall . I ts ability to spread quickly by rhizomes to form colonies and to thrive in moist to wet soils means that yellowroot can be a useful groundcover for banks and in streambank restoration. Although happy in moist to wet soils, it also tolerates drier conditions and dry shade . In the garden, yellowroot can be used as a shrubby deciduous groundcover in woodland , shady, and naturalized areas . However, because it will enthusiastically spread, its root run should be confined, e.g., metal edges or concrete if this spread is not desired. At Bedrock Gardens, yellowroot can be found in the Pond (#5) garden.

Woodland Poeny

Woodland Peony ( Paeonia obovata ) Native to China, Japan, and Korea, this species is a peony that lives in shady environments, unlike virtually all other peonies. Its lovely delicate white (or pink) flowers bloom in May-June and are followed in the fall by showy jester-hat shaped seedpods that burst open to reveal blue-black seeds nestled on a magenta interior. The seedpods add much interest to a woodland garden at a time when blooms are becoming scarce. At Bedrock Gardens, broad swaths of woodland peonies can be found in the Swaleway (#14) and Funnel (#13) gardens.

Shredded Umbrella Plant

Shredded Umbrella Plant ( Syneilesis aconitifloria ) ​ The shredded umbrella plant is a woodland perennial native to Japan, China, and Korea. Although it flowers later in the summer, it is most interesting in the spring when its white, wooly leaves emerge from the soil. Closely furled along its single stalk like an umbrella, the leaves look amazingly like an angry cat has attacked them and torn them to pieces. The rhizomes spread and gradually form a groundcover about 18” high of tattered 9-10” diameter small “umbrellas”. At Bedrock Gardens, shredded umbrella plants can be found in the Spiral Garden (#12) as well as along the Swaleway (#14) and Funnel (#13) gardens.

​Jill Nooney has Written a Book on Bedrock Gardens! ​

BEDROCK: The Making of a Public Garden by Jill Nooney

Foreword by Lee Buttala, preservationist for The Garden Conservancy Bedrock: The Making of a Public Garden is a love letter from a woman to the 30-acre garden that she and her husband spent 40 years creating in Lee, NH. Featuring almost 300 photographs of the garden, sculptures, water features, and built structures, the book takes us from the garden’s private origin to a public garden. ​ ​ Readers’ Comments “I have had the good fortune to visit many public gardens in North America and abroad. However, until spending time with Jill and Bob in the wonderful space that they brought to life, I had never experienced the visceral, electric energy of creation in action . I am humbled by their work and more important, their steadfast desire to share it with the world. Bedrock Gardens is as unique and magical as its creators-- a truly remarkable story of inspiration, whimsy, and beauty that will bring a smile to your soul !” —Bill Cullina, executive director of the Morris Arboretum and author of four books “More than a tale of building an artist’s garden, this is a love story of two people creating a haven of joy and solace. This book not only documents the development of the garden, but it also conveys Jill’s thinking and philosophy.” —Bill Thomas, executive director of Chanticleer Garden and author of The Art of Gardening “Anyone who’s ever met Jill Nooney knows in seconds that she walks on the wild side. She turns an unexpected, crazy idea into a fun, funny and joyful reality on her land. Bedrock Gardens invites visitors to inhabit one artist’s vision of the world—to explore it at their own pace and to laugh in delight at what they discover. ” —Julie Moir Messervy, principal of Julie Moir Messervy Design Studio and author of nine books. " I can’t recommend it highly enough ." "You’ll love this book." -- Pam Penick, photographer, blogger, and author of multiple books, . Read it here . " Rich with entertaining anecdotes of realizing improbable, visionary concepts that involved moving boulders, carrying trees and rocks on planes, ferreting out rare plant material, operating all manner of equipment, and managing thousands of visitors, this is a story of love, loss and generosity . " -- Helen Dumont, Midwest Book Review. Read it here . " I loved the book!" -- Henry Homeyer, The Gardening Guy. Website and articles here . ​ See a fun short Promo video of it HERE . About the Author Jill Nooney grew up in rural New Jersey. She attended Bennington College, Smith College School of Social Work, and the Radcliffe Seminars Program in Landscape Design. She has had a lifelong interest in plants, art, and healing the human spirit. She lives with her husband, Bob Munger, in an old farmhouse in New Hampshire. Together, over the span of forty years, they created Bedrock Gardens and, in 2023, they gifted the garden to the Friends of Bedrock Gardens to be enjoyed by all as a public garden.

VISITOR INFORMATION

Map and Directions

We are located in Lee, New Hampshire, halfway between Boston, MA, and Portland, ME, about 15 miles inland from the seacoast. The nearest town is Durham, home of the ﻿ University ﻿ of New Hampshire. 19 High Road ( physical address ) 45 High Road ( mailing address ) ​​ Lee, NH, 03861-6202

Driving Directions

From the South: Drive North on I-95. Take Exit 2 in NH (Exeter/Hampton), and go west on Route 101. After about 12 miles, take Exit 7 in Epping. Go north on Route 125 for about 5 miles. At the stop light at the intersection with Route 152, turn left onto Route 152 West (towards Nottingham). Take the next left (100 feet) onto High Road. Take your first left into Bedrock Gardens parking area. Town ordinances prohibit parking on the street. ​ From the North: Exit Route I-95 onto Route 4 (Spaulding turnpike). Follow Route 4 west. Don't miss the exit from the Spaulding turnpike onto Route 4 in about 4 miles. Go another 10 miles through Durham towards Concord. At the Lee Traffic Circle, turn left (South) onto Route 125. Go south on Route 125 for about 5 miles. At the stop light at the intersection with Route 152, turn right onto Route 152 West (towards Nottingham). Take the next left (100 feet) onto High Road. Take your first left into Bedrock Gardens parking area. Town ordinances prohibit parking on the street. From the West: Go East on Route 101. Take Exit 7 in Epping and go north on Route 125 for about 5 miles. At the stop light at the intersection with Route 152, turn left onto Route 152 West (towards Nottingham.) Take the next left (100 feet) onto High Road. Take your first left into Bedrock Gardens parking area. Town ordinances prohibit parking on the street.

​ ​ Notes: This sculpture ( The Tankard ) is at the intersection of Rtes. 125 and 152...and High Road! Keep the sculpture on your left. As you go down High Rd past this sculpture, we are the FIRST driveway on the left. If we are closed, there will be a sign there, and you can turn around there, and go back. ​Please don't turn around in the neighbor's driveways.
